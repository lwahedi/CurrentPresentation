Why do some groups form partnerships, but not others?

Why do groups partner when they do?

When material and strategic constraints are met.

The nature of the constraints depend on context

  • Ideologues in separate wars
  • Rivals in a single war
  • Transregional groups in local wars

Transregional Groups:

Operate in more than one contiguous region

AQIM operates in Algeria, and independent conflict in Mali.

PKK is transnational, but not transregional, because it operates in Kurdistan

PFLP was willing to train other groups, but did not want to a presence within their territory

Arguent in Brief

  • Asset Specificity
    • Transregional groups have global networks
    • Local groups have local infrastructure
    • Together: efficiency gains
  • Tensions in this context
    • Transregional groups want strong partners that can provide secure access to territory
    • Strong local groups don't want want to share their territory
  • When is sponsorship possible?
    • When the sovereignty costs of partnership are less than the benefits
    • When local groups can't get what they need for less

Broader Implications

Ideology

  • Groups partner with idoeologically similar groups
    • Beliefs
    • Legitimacy

  • Not enough to explain variation:
    • Outbidding: (Kydd & Walter)--Hezbollah & Amal, Nusra & ISIS
    • Timing: AQIM & al Qaeda, al Shabaab & al Qaeda
    • Failure to partner: al Qaeda in Palestine, Chechnya

Balance of Power

  • Alliances shift to form Minimum Winning Coalitions (Christia 2012)
  • Accounts for interdependence
  • Problems:
    • Ignores relative strength of individual partners
    • Limited to civil war context
    • Harder to flip sides without secure territory

Commitment Problems/ Principle Agent Problems)

  • Groups in civil war form asymmetric ties when state is strong because of commitment problems (Bapat & Bond 2013)
  • Weak groups trade autonomy for security (Morrow 1991)
  • Partners that are too strong can't be influenced (shapiro 2007)
  • Problems:
    • Controling partners without strong command & control is expensive (Shapiro 2008)
    • Can't project power without secure territory

Empirical Strategy

Evidence in favor

  • Transregional groups sponsor groups that provide secure access to territory, reject those that can't
  • Strong groups prefer independence
  • Tensions over independence persist after sponsorship
  • Local groups prefer resources with lower autonomy costs
  • Local groups accept sponsorship if:
    • access to resources is in decline
    • it shifts material power balance
    • it reduces dependence on potential rivals

Evidence Against

  • Transregional groups accept weak partners they can control
  • Civil war is Asymmetric: Strong local groups always want sponsorship too
  • Ideology alone describes partnership: transregional groups don't outbid
  • Transregional groups flip sides to support minimum winning coalitions
